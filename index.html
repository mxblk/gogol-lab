History
In 1970 Tsuchiya and Sumi reported of a new unidentified compound replacing the levoglucosan as a significant constituent on pyrolysis of acid retardant processed cellulose.(1) In 1973 the unidentified compound was reported to be cis-4,5-epoxy-2-pentenal (Figure 1-left) based on infrared (IR), nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and mass spectral analyses.(2) At the same time, Lipska and McCasland assigned 1,5-anhydro-2,3-dideoxy-β-d-pent-2-enofuranose structure to the same compound (Figure 1-right).(3)

However, the correct structure of the unknown compound was finally given by Helpern, Riffer and Broido.(4) They reported the phosphate salts catalysed pyrolysis of cellulose, collected volatiles and separated them by preparative gas chromatography. CHN, GC-MS, IR Spectroscopy, 13C and 1H NMR methods were used to identify the structure assigned to 1,6-anhydro-3,4-dideoxy-β-d-pyranosen-2-one (Figure 2) that is currently named as levoglucosenone.

Since the levoglucosenone structure was identified, the scientific world has started to look for applications for the new molecule. Applications Synthesis and medicine application The chiral nature of levoglucosenone (Figure 2) lends itself to the synthesis of natural and unnatural products. Some of the applications are reported in Table 1. More examples are available in the papers(5–9) and reviews(10–12).

Attractant for agriculture Another exciting use of levoglucosenone in natural product synthesis is the production of (+)-γ-pelargonolactone that is used as a flavouring agent and an attractant of rice and corn weevils Sitophiltus zaemais (Figure 3).(26)

Intermediate for the polymer industry Levoglucosenone has multiple applications as a precursor of 1,6- hexanediol and 1,2,6-hexanetriol. These are key intermediates in the industrial synthesis of 1,6-hexanediamine, caprolactone and caprolactam, which are used for the manufacturing of polyester, polyamide and polyurethanes, representing multimillion operations.(27–29)

Historical overview
The first mention of levoglucosan is dated in 1894 by C. Tarnet, who was treating picein, salicin and coniferin (Figure 1) in baryte-containing water at 100°C. He named a new compound “levoglucosan” because of the levorotation properties to distinguish it from the potential dextrorotation isomer - the glucosan, published earlier by A. Gelis.(1) Some authors assumed levoglucosan being a result β-glucose transformations.(2)

Pictet and Sarasin reported the formation of levoglusan in the yield of 30wt% in pyrolysis of cotton cellulose. (1) High yield of LGA indicates a vital role of this molecule in pyrolysis processes. Parks and co-workers made a clear statement of levoglucosan playing a crucial role in cellulose pyrolysis in 1955.(3) Shafizadeh assumed levoglucosan being a principle intermediate in cellulose pyrolysis.(4)

Biopolymers
Unsubstituted levoglucosan polymerises either thermally,(5–7) or in the presence of acid catalysts such as monochloroacetic acid(8) to give low molecular weight oligo- or polysaccharides. The strained cyclic ether ring in one levoglucosan molecule reacts with anyone of the three hydroxyls in another. The strained cyclic ether in the resulting dimer then reacts with any hydroxyl in a third molecule and so on. Predictably if eaten, these complex glucose-based polymers would not be fully digested by humans. Like Pfizer's "Polydextrose" produced from sugar using an enzymatic process, they might find applications as non-fattening fillers in low calorie foods.(9) By analogy to other natural branched chain polysaccharides, including guar gum, complex polymers resulting from levoglucosan self-polymerisation might be expected to be useful water thickening agents. As such they would find applications as rheological agents in enhanced oil recovery operations which involve water flooding of wells. Likewise, reacting levoglucosan with starch or cellulose could also produce branched chain polymers having similar useful properties. The literature describes polyethers prepared from levoglucosan and alkylene oxides in the presence of basic catalysts. These derivatives can be used as polyols and reacted with isocyanates to produce polyurethane foams.(10) Polyethers prepared from levoglucosan and allyl bromide can be converted into polyfunctional epoxy intermediates. These in turn are said to find applications as epoxy resins.

Biodegradable plastics
A biodegradable plastic based on biomass-derived levoglucosan is an attractive concept. The chemical incorporation of levoglucosan into polymers is expected to give a biodegradable plastic in which extensive polymer chain degradation will occur.(9) There is exist a ring-opening polymerisation of levoglucosan derivatives for the preparation of synthetic polysaccharides, including dextrans (Figure 2). The protective alkyl group, R, can be removed to generate a dextran, can be chosen to allow subsequent branching of the polysaccharide or can be a long chain hydrocarbon to generate an amphiphilic polysaccharide.(9) The incorporation of a monosaccharide such as levoglucosan into a polymeric structure can impart the following properties to the polymer: hydrophilicity, biodegradability and optical activity (chirality).

Chiral catalysts incorporating levoglucosan-based ligands
Levoglucosan is a structurally rigid chiral molecule with five asymmetric carbon centres; this has made it an ideal chiral building block in the synthesis of very complex asymmetric molecules,(11) such as antibiotics,(12) milbemycins and avermectins (13) (insecticides and antiparasitic agents), and thromboxanes.(14) Examples of chiral compounds prepared industrially via asymmetric synthesis are L-DOPA, which is active against Parkinson's disease, the excellent anti-inflammatory drug, Naproxen, and the sweetener, aspartame, which is a dipeptide. There is much industrial interest in efficient methods of asymmetric synthesis, especially in manufacture of pharmaceuticals, flavours, fragrances and pesticides (including pheromones and insect hormones).(9) It is possible to produce chiral phosphine and phosphinite ligands from levoglucosan. The ligands are used to prepare chiral rhodium catalysts, e.g. Figure 3, which will be tested in asymmetric hydrogenation and other asymmetric transformations.

Intermediate for valuable compounds, such as glucosides, levoglucosenone, 5-hydroxymethylfurfural, levulinic acid, furfural and others. Levoglucosan has been reacted with higher alcohols containing up to 25 carbon atoms, in the presence of acid catalysts to give alkyl monoglucosides. Glucosides containing more than one glucoside unit are also formed.(15) The glucosides are said to be advantageously used as gelling agents, lubricants, wetting agents, dyeing assistants, textile softeners and food emulsifiers. It therefore appears that there is little advantage to using what must always be more expensive levoglucosan to prepare these glucosides. If levoglucosan is a principle intermediate in cellulose and starch pyrolysis, then it can originate all cellulose and starch pyro-chemicals in precise conditions.
Medical application
The use of levoglucosan is disclosed as a pharmaceutical active agent especially for the treatment of infectious or inflammatory conditions. It is particularly useful for the treatment of Herpes Simplex and Herpes Zoster.(16)

Historical Overview
Melanin is a complex polymer derived from the amino acid tyrosine. Melanin is responsible for determining skin and hair colour and is present in the skin to varying degrees, depending on how much a population has been exposed to the sun historically.(1) Melanin synthesis Numerous steps are involved in the biosynthesis of melanin. The first step is catalysis of the chemical L-3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine by tyrosinase. A lack of tyrosine can lead to albinism. Tyrosine is only found in a specialized cells called melanocytes, inside which tiny granules of melanin pigment are contained in vesicles called melanosomes.(1)
